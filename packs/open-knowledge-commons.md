# Open Knowledge and the Knowledge Commons

## A working definition

Open knowledge is knowledge that people can access, understand, reuse, adapt, and share under clear legal and technical conditions. A knowledge commons is not merely a pile of free files. It is a shared resource plus the community practices that keep it discoverable, trustworthy, inclusive, and maintainable.

## Four layers of a healthy commons

- **Content:** explanations, data, sources, translations, and teaching materials.
- **Infrastructure:** repositories, formats, identifiers, backups, and search.
- **Governance:** rules for contribution, review, attribution, correction, and removal.
- **People:** learners, educators, domain experts, translators, maintainers, and local partners.

## What openness does not mean

Openness does not erase privacy, consent, safety, accessibility needs, cultural rights, or the license of third-party work. A responsible project publishes less when publication would expose a person to harm, and it explains that boundary.

## Project practice

Every item should carry an author or team, date, sources, license, review status, and a route for correction. Prefer small composable files over an unreviewable document dump.
