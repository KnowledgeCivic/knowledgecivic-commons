# How to Evaluate a Public Claim

## The civic problem

Public decisions depend on claims about what happened, what is likely, and what should be done. These are different kinds of claims. A useful first move is to ask which kind you are looking at instead of arguing immediately about the conclusion.

## A seven-question method

1. **What exactly is being claimed?** Rewrite slogans as a testable sentence.
2. **What kind of claim is it?** Separate observation, prediction, causal explanation, value judgment, and policy proposal.
3. **What would count as evidence?** Define the relevant data before searching for confirming examples.
4. **Who produced the evidence?** Check methods, incentives, expertise, date, geography, sample, and missing information.
5. **Can the source be traced?** Prefer the original dataset, law, study, budget, transcript, or official record over screenshots and reposts.
6. **What would change our mind?** State a falsifier or an uncertainty range.
7. **What is the decision at stake?** A true fact does not automatically determine one policy; values and trade-offs must be made explicit.

## Common failure modes

- Mistaking repetition for corroboration.
- Treating a source's authority as proof of every sentence it publishes.
- Using a dramatic anecdote to answer a population-level question.
- Ignoring base rates, denominators, time windows, or comparison groups.
- Presenting a false balance when evidence is strongly asymmetric.
- Treating uncertainty as ignorance or as permission to believe anything.
